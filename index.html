About Me

I am currently a member of the VLSI Design and Test Lab at SDSU where I design digital circuits targeting FPGA and ASIC platforms. My PhD co-advisors are Professor Amir Alimohammad (SDSU) and Professor Patrick Mercier (UCSD).

My research focuses on the implementation of brain-computer interfaces (BCIs), particularly how we can design scalable wireless BCIs for future implantable use. I study a variety of topics, ranging from digital circuit design to machine-learning based neural decoding. I've also had the pleasure of being the teaching assistant for various digital logic courses at SDSU throughout my studies.

Recent Publications


Binarized Neural Networks for Efficient Spike Sorting

In this article, we expand on the application of Binarized Neural Networks (BNNs) for efficient spike sorting. To combat the effects of noise on the shape and subsequent classification of a neural action potential, we allow certain layers and parameters of the network model more resolution compared to the conventional BNNs.

In Vivo Spike Detection with Adaptive Noise Estimation

In this article, we study the impact of various signal processing algorithms on the ability to reliably detect neural action potentials from intra-cortical recordings, including filtering, signal pre-emphasis, and noise estimation methods.

Toward In Vivo Neural Decoding

In this article, we study the feasibility of a custom-designed in vivo decoding processor for implementing machine learning-based decoding algorithms in real-time.

Neural Spike Sorting Using Binarized Neural Networks

In this article, we presented the application of binarized neural networks, which are neural networks with their weights constrained to either +1 or -1, in an effort to reduce the computational complexity and memory requirements for realizing in-vivo real-time spike sorting.
